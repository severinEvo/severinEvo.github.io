new paper published

2021-01-12

My latest paper just got published! In this collaborative work between former lab grad student Jake Gockley and me, we used two rounds of Massively Parallel Reporter Assays to test human-specific substitutions in candidate enhancer elements for their regulatory effect in a brain development cell model. Testing over 32,000 substitutions in more than 4300 candidate enhancers, we found 401 substitutions with quantifiable effects on regulatory activity. Read more on how substitutions interact to produce a modified activity and how they alter transcription factor binding sites at PNAS: Massively parallel discovery of human-specific substitutions that alter enhancer activity.

big preprint release

2019-12-19

The Noonan lab just posted three preprints on bioRxiv, all three with my contributions. One of them presents the major part of my postdoc work as well as a main part of Jake Gockley's PhD thesis. In this study, we used Massively Parallel Reporter Assays to screen over 32,000 human-specific enhancer substitutions for their regulatory effects in human neural stem cells. Here is the preprint on bioRxiv: Massively parallel discovery of human-specific substitutions that alter neurodevelopmental enhancer activity.

In addition, we posted Evan Geller's PhD project, where he used CRISPR to disrupt thousands of genes and enhancers to uncover their effects on cell proliferation (Massively parallel disruption of enhancers active during human corticogenesis) and Emily Dutrow's PhD project, where she uncovered the regulatory effects of a Human Accelerated Region using humanized and primatized mice (The Human Accelerated Region HACNS1 modifies developmental gene expression in humanized mice).

new preprint

2018-08-27

I just posted a preprint that results from a little project that I took on. I used two challenging datasets, a developmental time course and combined data from different source labs, to evaluate RNA-seq normalization methods. It turned out that these tasks were indeed so tricky to normalise for that most, if not all, normalization methods did not perform particularly well. The paper is formatted as a short report, so it is a quick read. Here is the preprint on bioRxiv: Evaluation of RNA-seq normalization methods using challenging datasets.

new paper out

2018-06-02 (posted 2018-07-22)

In my latest paper, my colleagues and I take a next step in analysing allele-specific gene expression. We present RPASE, a method that detects allele-specific expression with nothing more but aligned RNA-seq reads and a list of SNPs from your favourite organism. No genome sequence needed, an assembled transcriptome sequence is sufficient to create this input data. Try it out and let us know what you think! Here's a link to the GitHub repository: wangmi811/RPASE. And here's the paper, published in Molecular Ecology Resources: RPASE: Individual‐based allele‐specific expression detection without prior knowledge of haplotype phase.

new paper out

2018-03-16

The bioRxiv preprint we posted last year has now been published in Development! My colleague Richard Sarro deleted the single most important limb enhancer for the Pitx1 gene and found no compensatory effects in the changed regulatory topology of the locus. No other enhancers increased their activity, no shadow enhancers found! Development was normal nonetheless! The paper is now available in online early version: Disrupting the three-dimensional regulatory topology of the Pitx1 locus results in overtly normal development. Here is the link to the bioRxiv preprint.

Deleting the Pitx1 enhancer does not alter limb development in a detectable way.
